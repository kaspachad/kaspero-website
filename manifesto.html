The Kaspa Manifesto

Infrastructure Before Hype
A sober vision for a real crypto economy

Crypto was supposed to change the world.

It was supposed to replace money - not build bridges back to fiat.

It was supposed to create a new economic layer - not become a casino where speculation masquerades as innovation.

We can still build that future. But we need to ask harder questions first.

Look around the crypto space and you'll see incredible technical talent building impressive solutions:

  • Elegant cross-chain protocols with cutting-edge cryptography
  • Sophisticated DeFi mechanisms that would make traditional finance jealous
  • Novel consensus algorithms that push the boundaries of distributed systems
  • Smart contract platforms optimized for every conceivable use case

But here's the uncomfortable question: Who are we building for?

Too often, the honest answer is "ourselves" - other developers, other protocols, other tokens. We've created an ecosystem where the most common users are builders, and the most common use case is building more infrastructure.

Meanwhile, the foundational pieces of a real crypto economy remain unbuilt:

  • Storefronts where people can actually buy things with crypto
  • Labor marketplaces where work is paid in crypto, not converted to fiat
  • Point-of-sale systems that small businesses can actually use
  • Consumer experiences that don't require a computer science degree

The race to be first vs. the race to be useful

There's nothing wrong with wanting to be first. Innovation requires risk-taking and bold moves. But being first at building something no one needs is just expensive performance art.

The real question isn't "Can we build this?" It's "Should we build this?"

How can a normal person live their life using Kaspa without ever touching a bank?

That question should guide every technical decision. Not "What's the most elegant solution?" but "What gets us closer to that world?"

We need:

  • Commerce platforms before more DEX innovations
  • User-friendly wallets before wallet-to-wallet protocols
  • Real storefronts before more token standards
  • Practical adoption tools before theoretical scaling solutions

The developer trap we've all fallen into

We've created a feedback loop where:

  • Developers build for developers because that's the only audience that exists
  • The only users are other developers because that's the only audience we're building for
  • Funding goes to projects that impress other developers, not ones that serve real needs
  • Success is measured by technical achievement, not real-world impact

This isn't malicious. It's natural. But it's also a trap that keeps crypto from reaching its potential.

Breaking out requires a different kind of courage - the courage to build boring, useful things instead of impressive, unused things.

What would a real crypto economy look like?

Imagine if crypto worked like the internet:

  • Your barista gets paid in KAS and uses it to buy groceries
  • Small businesses accept crypto because it's easier than traditional payments
  • People save and spend in crypto because it's more convenient than fiat
  • The economic infrastructure just works, invisibly, in the background

This isn't about ideology. It's about utility. Money should be a tool that makes life easier, not a puzzle that requires solving.

The path there isn't through more financial primitives. It's through making crypto useful for the 99% of the population who don't care about financial primitives.

A different kind of building

This doesn't mean abandoning innovation. It means directing our considerable talents toward problems that matter to people who aren't us.

It means asking "What do regular people need?" before asking "What can the technology do?"

It means building bridges to the real world, not just bridges between blockchains.

The technical challenges are just as hard. The difference is that solving them actually changes lives.

We're not here to build the most impressive technology.
We're here to build the most useful one.
- Someone who still believes in the original vision
